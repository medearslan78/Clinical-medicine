Chronic Liver Disease (CLD)
🫀
Common Causes (ABCDE)
  • Alcohol — #1 in West; Hep B/C — #1 in Pakistan
  • NAFLD / NASH (metabolic syndrome)
  • Wilson's, Hemochromatosis, AIH
Child-Pugh Score Variables
  • Bilirubin, Albumin, PT/INR
  • Ascites, Hepatic Encephalopathy
  • A (5-6) = Compensated; B (7-9); C (10-15) = Decompensated
Complications (CAVE-SH)
  • Coagulopathy, Ascites, Varices, Encephalopathy
  • SBP, Hepatorenal syndrome, HCC

Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD)
🫘
Staging (GFR-based)
  • Stage 1: GFR ≥90 (with kidney damage marker)
  • Stage 3: GFR 30-59 | Stage 4: GFR 15-29
  • Stage 5: GFR <15 = ESRD → Dialysis/Transplant
Complications (ABCDE-F)
  • Anemia (EPO↓), Bone disease (PTH↑, Ca↓, PO4↑)
  • CVD, Dyslipidemia, Edema / HTN, Fluid overload
Dialysis Indications (AEIOU)
  • Acidosis, Electrolytes (hyperK), Intoxication
  • Overload (fluid), Uremia symptoms

Acute Coronary Syndrome
❤️
Classification
  • UA: No troponin rise, transient ECG changes
  • NSTEMI: Troponin ↑, No ST elevation
  • STEMI: ST elevation + Troponin ↑ → PCI <90 min
Initial Rx (MONA-B)
  • Morphine, O2 (if SpO2 <94%), Nitrates, Aspirin 325mg
  • Beta-blocker (if no contraindication)
  • Heparin + P2Y12 inhibitor (Clopidogrel/Ticagrelor)
High-Yield ECG Changes
  • Hyperacute T waves → ST elevation → Q waves → T inversion
  • Posterior MI: ST depression V1-V4 (reciprocal)

Rheumatoid Arthritis (RA)
🦴
ACR 2010 Criteria (Score ≥6)
  • Joint involvement, Serology (RF / Anti-CCP)
  • Acute-phase reactants (CRP/ESR), Duration ≥6 wk
Extra-Articular Features
  • Rheumatoid nodules, Pulmonary fibrosis
  • Felty's syndrome, Scleritis, Vasculitis
  • Caplan's syndrome (pneumoconiosis)
Management Pyramid
  • NSAIDs → Hydroxychloroquine / Sulfasalazine
  • Methotrexate (gold standard DMARD)
  • Biologics: Anti-TNF (Adalimumab, Etanercept)

SLE (Systemic Lupus)
🦋
SLICC Criteria (4 of 11+)
  • Malar rash, Photosensitivity, Oral ulcers, Alopecia
  • Arthritis (≥2 joints), Serositis, Renal, Neuro
  • Hemolytic anemia, Leukopenia, Thrombocytopenia
Key Antibodies
  • ANA — most sensitive (95%); not specific
  • Anti-dsDNA — most specific; tracks disease activity
  • Anti-Sm — specific; Anti-Ro/La — Neonatal lupus
Mx
  • Hydroxychloroquine — all patients
  • Steroids + Azathioprine / MMF for flares
  • Cyclophosphamide — Lupus nephritis class III/IV

Tuberculosis (TB)
🦠
Diagnosis
  • Sputum AFB ×3, Culture (Gold standard), GeneXpert
  • TST (Mantoux) + IGRA (Quantiferon-TB)
  • CXR: Upper lobe cavitation, Ghon complex
Treatment (RIPE)
  • 2 months Intensive: Rifampicin + INH + PZA + Ethambutol
  • 4 months Continuation: Rifampicin + INH
  • MDR-TB: Bedaquiline, Linezolid, Levofloxacin
Side Effects (Mnemonic RIPE)
  • R: Hepatotoxicity, Orange urine; I: Peripheral neuropathy
  • P: Hyperuricemia; E: Optic neuritis

High-Yield MCQs
Based on USMLE Step 1 & 2 + UHS Final MBBS style questions
Nephrology USMLE Step 2
A 65-year-old diabetic patient has GFR of 18 mL/min, serum potassium 6.8 mEq/L, pH 7.18, and is oliguric with signs of fluid overload. Which is the MOST appropriate next step?
AIV Furosemide 80 mg
BRestrict dietary potassium
CInitiate emergency hemodialysis
DIV sodium bicarbonate infusion
✅ C — Emergency Hemodialysis. This patient meets multiple AEIOU indications: severe Acidosis (pH 7.18), life-threatening hyperkalemia (6.8), fluid Overload unresponsive to diuretics, and Uremia. Furosemide is unlikely to work in ESRD. Dialysis is the definitive, fastest intervention.
Hepatology High Yield
A 45-year-old male presents with hematemesis. Endoscopy shows grade III esophageal varices with active bleeding. After initial resuscitation, which pharmacological agent should be given IMMEDIATELY?
AOmeprazole IV infusion
BTerlipressin (or Octreotide)
CFresh Frozen Plasma
DPropranolol orally
✅ B — Terlipressin / Octreotide. Vasoactive drugs reduce portal pressure and should be started as soon as variceal bleed is suspected — BEFORE endoscopy if possible. Terlipressin (or Somatostatin/Octreotide) is the drug of choice. Propranolol is for prophylaxis, not acute bleed. PPI is for peptic ulcer bleed.
Rheumatology USMLE Step 1
Which antibody is MOST specific for Systemic Lupus Erythematosus (SLE) and correlates with disease activity?
AANA (Anti-nuclear antibody)
BAnti-Smith (Anti-Sm)
CAnti-dsDNA
DAnti-Ro (SSA)
✅ C — Anti-dsDNA. Anti-dsDNA is highly specific for SLE AND tracks with disease activity (flares = rising titres, low complement C3/C4). Anti-Sm is specific but does NOT track activity. ANA is most sensitive (95%) but not specific. Anti-Ro causes neonatal lupus and congenital heart block.
Infectious Disease High Yield
A patient presents with high-grade fever, relative bradycardia (Faget's sign), rose spots on abdomen, and constipation in week 1. Widal titre is 1:160 (O antigen). What is the drug of choice?
AAmoxicillin 500mg TDS
BChloramphenicol
CCeftriaxone IV
DCiprofloxacin
✅ C — Ceftriaxone IV. This is Typhoid (Enteric Fever) caused by Salmonella typhi. Ceftriaxone is the current drug of choice for uncomplicated and complicated typhoid (especially where fluoroquinolone resistance is common in South Asia). Chloramphenicol is historical. Widal >1:160 with O-antigen is significant.
